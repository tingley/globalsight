Web globalization - Isn't the Web already global?

The World Wide Web is a medium that can be accessed by users from just about any place in the world. However, global accessibility is not synonymous with global communication. For the medium to be truly global it must communicate in the languages and cultures of its users. Today, over 50% of the online community originates from outside the US. By 2005, non-US Web users are forecast to comprise 700 million of the total one billion users.

Web site globalization is about ensuring that a Web site is understood, appreciated, and effectively used by the majority of the people accessing it. At a minimum, this means that your Web site should be in the languages of your target audience and be reviewed by your overseas affiliates to ensure conformity with applicable laws and prevailing cultures.


What are the major challenges with respect to Web content globalization?

By speaking with many companies which are investigating and/or implementing multilingual Web sites, Global Sight has found that the largest issues facing business leaders with respect to Web content globalization are as follows:

  • Organizational coordination and process buy-in;
  • Creating consistent global messages and enforceable global branding while ensuring that messages are culturally attuned to their respective foreign markets;
  • Multilingual content synchronization – keeping multilingual content in synch with the source language Web site; and
  • Multilingual content depth and freshness - Web users expect to see more than a few token Web pages translated into their native language, and the content must be current to keep them coming back.

Upon closer analysis of these issues, Global Sight has learned that all of these issues are actually a subset of the first (organizational coordination and process buy-in). It is impossible to effectively deal with any subset issues without the development and implementation of processes which are easily understood and universally accepted throughout the organization.


How can workflow software really make a difference with respect to Web globalization?

Web-enabled workflow software automates the flow of information, streamlines communication and provides a current status report of translation/review jobs. In turn, this streamlined process greatly reduces multilingual content development cycles and site production costs. The alternative is a manual process.


Describe the “manual process” of Web globalization?

Web globalization is an ongoing process that requires coordinating the activities of multiple teams located around the world to develop, translate, review and approve multilingual Web content. The manual process involves the painstaking task of cutting/pasting multilingual content to and from HTML pages, sending out files for translation/review and attempting to coordinate the activities of a globally distributed team via phone, fax or email.

Updates on the status of pending jobs are obtained by late night phone calls or emails, as project managers are not provided with real-time job status information. Therefore, bottlenecks at one stage in the translation/review process may go unnoticed until deadlines are missed. As the global site grows and more languages are added, the increased complexity leads to a greater risk of communication breakdowns, long delays and rising costs to maintain the site.


What is the difference between Internationalization, Globalization and Localization as they relate to Web sites?

Localization (L10N), is the process by which the Web site content and ‘look and feel’ are developed to meet the needs of a particular audience -- graphics and/or wording are tailored for a specific market.

Internationalization (I18N) is the process by which the site content and ‘look and feel’ are developed to accommodate and simplify multilingual and multicultural localization--graphics, placement of text, development of paragraphs, hyperlinks, special text, etc.

Globalization (G11N) is the process by which site development, update processes, and workflow have been engineered to provide a robust framework for cost-effective multilingual site development and maintenance--incorporating overseas offices, consultants, etc.

What is text extraction – and why is it needed?

Web pages contain a combination of content and HTML code. Code determines the presentation of a Web page while content is the visible text. In order to make the content of a Web page easily translatable and to enable a streamlined review process, it is beneficial to separate the code from the content. The separation of code from the content is often referred to as text extraction.

Placement of this extracted content into a database serves as the foundation for a multilingual content management system. Other benefits of a multilingual content management solution include version control, automated workflow, and content editing capabilities.

What are double-byte characters?

Double-byte character encoding is an encoding which is necessary for representing non-alphabet-based languages such as Chinese, Japanese, and Korean on a computer.

Alphabet-based languages such as English and French are able to create a great number of words from a small set of characters. In these languages it is possible to use fewer than 256 symbols (A-Z lower/upper case, 0-9, and accents) to create thousands of words. The number 256 is significant because it is the number of objects which can be stored in one byte of data.

However, character-based languages like Chinese and Japanese do not have alphabets. They require thousands of pieces of information to capture the language. As thousands of pieces of information cannot be stored in a single computer byte, computer scientists have opted to store these character sets under a double-byte encoding scheme (two bytes, or a double-byte encoding system, can store upwards of 65,000 separate pieces of information).

As is clear, there is nothing inherently difficult about the concept of a double-byte character. However, most software to date has not been developed with the ability to process double-byte characters. Serious problems may arise if these systems are adopted to process double-byte character set information.
